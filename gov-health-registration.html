Smarter Starts in Healthcare

A look at how user research revealed key onboarding challenges for Australian government healthcare and pointed the way to meaningful improvements.

Overview

An independent UX case study evaluating the onboarding experience of myGov through a user-centred lens, with a focus on usability, accessibility, security, and inclusivity.

It examines key onboarding flows including account creation, sign-in, and account recovery, along with modern authentication methods like Digital ID and passkeys, assessing alignment with Australian Government design standards.

As Australia continues to lead in government technology adoption*, improving these first touchpoints is critical to reducing friction, increasing completion rates, and enabling more users to access essential services.



myGov is the Australian Government’s central digital platform, providing secure access to a range of essential services including Medicare, Centrelink, and the Australian Taxation Office.

Source: Digital Tranformation Agency (DTA)

Goal

The primary goal of this study is to identify usability barriers, accessibility gaps, and points of friction within the onboarding experience of myGov. It focuses on how these issues impact users’ ability to confidently and securely create, access, and recover their accounts across diverse demographics and authentication methods.

Success Criteria

A successful onboarding experience enables users to:

  • Understand the purpose of myGov and what is required to get started
  • Navigate account creation, sign-in, and recovery flows with ease, regardless of technical ability, language, or background
  • Authenticate successfully using their preferred method, including Digital ID, passkeys, or traditional login
  • Feel confident that their personal information is secure and their privacy is protected
  • Complete onboarding independently without confusion, errors, or accessibility barriers

Identifying the Problem

User research methods and strategy

The initial step was to examine similar digital products. This allowed me to see how other airlines are solving the same problem and, more importantly, helped deepen my own understanding.

My research strategy used triangulation: a mix of qualitative and quantitative methods. This allowed me to look at the problem from multiple perspectives and validate my findings.

Interviews

Stakeholder Interviews

An interview with a former UX Lead from the Australian Digital Health Agency provided insight into the systemic and organisational factors shaping onboarding experiences in government platforms such as myGov. The discussion explored how trust, security, policy, and operational constraints influence UX decisions within highly regulated digital services.


Key Findings

  • Consistent government branding plays an important role in building user trust
  • Privacy and security requirements heavily influence onboarding and authentication flows
  • Policy constraints and procurement processes can limit design flexibility and slow iteration
  • Cross-agency collaboration impacts the consistency of digital service experiences
  • Onboarding is a critical trust-building moment where clarity and simplicity are essential

User Interviews

A series of user interviews were conducted with participants from diverse backgrounds to better understand experiences and perceptions of the onboarding process for myGov. The interviews explored how users interpreted onboarding flows, authentication methods, and system language across varying levels of digital literacy and cultural background.


Key Findings

  • Digital literacy and cultural background influenced how participants interpreted the onboarding journey
  • Authentication terminology, particularly around Digital ID and passkeys, was often unclear or unfamiliar
  • Participants preferred simple, direct language during account creation and sign-in
  • Government digital services were commonly perceived as more complex than expected
  • Unfamiliar technical terminology reduced confidence early in the onboarding process
  • Clear communication and plain language were identified as essential for supporting user confidence and accessibility

Takeaway: The interviews revealed that effective onboarding in government services depends on balancing strong security and policy requirements with clear, accessible, and user-centred communication. Across both stakeholders and users, clarity, simplicity, inclusivity, and trust emerged as critical factors in reducing friction and supporting successful account access.

Competitive Benchmarking

Competitive Benchmarking Study

A competitive benchmarking review was conducted across 3 (three) government platforms and 1 (one) banking application to evaluate onboarding patterns, authentication methods, and communication approaches within secure digital services.

The review identified a growing shift toward centralised “one-stop-shop” ecosystems using single sign-on (SSO) gateways to access multiple services. Across all platforms, security was prioritised through multi-factor authentication and one-time passwords (OTP).

Key opportunities for improvement included more flexible sign-in options, such as mobile-based login, passkeys, and PIN-based authentication as alternatives to traditional passwords. Communication throughout onboarding was also often overly formal and text-heavy, while terms and conditions were frequently presented as dense blocks of text that increased cognitive load and reduced readability.

Additional friction points included poor system feedback during onboarding and sign-in, intrusive pop-ups that disrupted user flow, and limited guidance when users were unable to self-recover their passwords or regain account access.

Takeaway: The benchmarking highlighted the need to balance security and compliance with more human-centred communication, clearer system feedback, and more accessible onboarding experiences.

Articulating the Problem

Making sense of the research through analysis

To synthesize findings from usability testing, online surveys, and competitive benchmarking, I conducted an affinity diagramming exercise.

The exercise revealed patterns in user behavior and perceptions, enabling our team to pinpoint key opportunities based on actual user feedback and, ultimately, define a clear UX problem statement.

Journey Mapping

Affinity Diagram: Identifying Themes/Patterns

Collaborating with another UX researcher, we independently analyzed the research data. Afterward, we consolidated our findings in Miro, organizing the insights into four (4) key themes.


Positive & Negative Sentiment:
Users appreciated smooth navigation but were frustrated by slow booking flows and inconsistent UI elements.


Clarity of Communication:
Terms like “fare class” confused users, while others valued the simplicity of "return flights" and "review and pay".


Mental Models:
Several users expected key features—such as filtering—to be more easily accessible, and anticipated that flight listings would default to sorting by departure time.


Pain Points:
A frequent pain point for users was the challenge of finding essential flight details, particularly stopover information in relation to overall flight duration.


Takeaway: Affinity diagramming helped me transform raw data into organized insights. The resulting thematic clusters informed the user journey map, shaped the problem definition, and guided subsequent design decisions.


Next Up: Designing a User-centered Solution

The UX Problem

Solving the Problem

Designing a user-centered solution

With the UX problem clearly defined, I began ideating interaction design solutions and outlining the UX flow—a system of tasks a user performs.

I followed up by sketching early design concepts, which helped shape the layout, content hierarchy, and overall user journey.

UX Flow Diagram

The goal was to design a complete and intuitive user flow (Miro) that aligned both with user expectations and business objectives, while minimizing friction throughout the experience.

To achieve this, I focused on removing unnecessary steps that could confuse or frustrate users and refining micro-interactions. Through this approach, I identified opportunities to streamline interactions and reduce complexity.

  • For example, if a user selects a one-way trip, the return date field is hidden, eliminating irrelevant input.
  • I ensured a logical progression through the booking process to avoid unnecessary back-and-forth, such as toggling between flight selection and seat selection screens.

I also introduced ways to improve the overall UX by allowing users to skip non-mandatory sections if they were not relevant to their needs.

Takeaway: Through iterative design of the UX flow, I reduced user interactions by 8% (from 37 to 34 touchpoints). Usability testing of the prototype validated that this streamlined flow can improve efficiency, with users showing faster task completion and fewer errors.

Note: For educational purposes, the "user easy path" focuses solely on the most direct booking path.


Next Step: Sketches and Wireframing

UX Flow - Miro Animation

Sketches

Next, I created a series of low-fidelity sketches and wireframes to accelerate decision-making through rapid visualization. These sketches outlined the key screens and controls for the booking process and laid the foundation for a functional prototype.

I began by defining the Information Architecture (IA), which provided the foundation for screen structure, content hierarchy, and consistent language. My initial sketches were quick and iterative, allowing me to quickly explore layout concepts and interaction patterns. This approach resulted in a structured, bare-bones low-fidelity interface that captured core functionality without the distraction of visual details.

User feedback highlighted the value of clear visual cues—especially progress bars, labeled steps, and prominent CTAs. I incorporated these into the sketches to improve clarity and build user confidence, offering a replicable approach for multi-step experiences.

With the core layout in place, I integrated the predefined user flow to ensure each screen supported a clear, seamless task progression. User interviews showed that reducing the number of fields per screen reduced cognitive load and abandonment rates, so I designed layouts with minimalism in mind—focusing on one task per screen to streamline the experience.

I reused sketch templates and applied overlay cut-outs, which helped save time and effort. I learned that I could easily sketch, modify, or discard ideas allowing for quick iterations.

Takeaway: I came back to the sketches throughout the entire design process. The sketches acted as a living reference to ensure that the evolving UI remained grounded in user behavior, task flows, and validated design principles.

Validation

Testing the solution

Following the development of the prototype, I entered a focused iteration phase. I led usability testing centered on key tasks, with specific attention to the identified UX problems and how effectively the new design addressed them.

Observing real users validated earlier design assumptions and revealed remaining issues related to layout, clarity, and flow. Using this feedback, I refined the design to deliver a more intuitive and user-centered experience.

Usability Testing 2.0

I conducted usability testing with two (2) new participants. The testing focused on the effectiveness of the system design/UI, and validating design changes aimed at resolving five critical UX issues: missing flight information, intrusive pop-ups, unclear pricing, user flow drop-off, and excessive upsell content.

Testing revealed clear improvements:

  • Users quickly found essential flight details that were previously overlooked
  • Intrusive pop-ups were revised or removed, resulting in a smoother experience
  • Pricing was easier to understand with consistent updates and clearer breakdowns
  • The updated flow reduced user hesitation and drop-off points
  • Upsell content was better integrated and less overwhelming

Final refinements improved layout and navigation. The progress bar’s arrow caused confusion by implying users were a step ahead. To resolve this, the current step was changed to a neutral capsule shape, and previous steps were adjusted for clarity with reduced opacity and increased contrast, enhancing overall usability.


Next steps: Wrap Up: Learnings & Next Steps

Design Iteration

Learnings

A key takeaway is the importance of centering user goals in both design and research. Too often, user intent—like quickly finding the right flight at the right price—is assumed rather than deeply understood.

This process reinforced that user goals must be actively explored through research and not be overlooked or treated as secondary.

For something as universally goal-driven as booking a flight, even small barriers can create frustration or drop-off. By grounding decisions in real user needs, we’re better equipped to build experiences that feel seamless, relevant, and ultimately more successful.

Next Steps

With major usability issues addressed, the next phase is about refining the experience further.

I'll move non-flight extras—like car rentals and travel insurance—to the end of the booking flow, after payment.

This keeps the core journey focused and friction-free, while still offering add-ons at a more appropriate moment.

I will also be looking at ways to further minimize touchpoints across the process—streamlining interactions without sacrificing clarity. The aim is to reduce cognitive load and maintain a fast, intuitive flow from start to finish.
